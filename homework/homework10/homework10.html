1. Context and Goal

This homework is conceptually connected to two previous activities:

  • the simulation of a Bernoulli process and the Law of Large Numbers (LLN),
  • the simulation of a server security random walk (Homework 7), where weekly outcomes are \((+1)\) for secure and \((-1)\) for breached weeks.

The aim of this homework is to:

  • discuss analogies and differences between the simple Bernoulli LLN simulation and the server random walk,
  • highlight how both are based on the same probabilistic structure,
  • illustrate the relationships with binomial coefficients, Pascal’s triangle, binomial expansion, the Fibonacci sequence and related combinatorial coefficients.

2. Bernoulli Process and Law of Large Numbers

In the LLN homework, we considered a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials:

\[ Y_1, Y_2, \dots, Y_n,\quad Y_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p), \]

where each trial represents a success (1) with probability \(p\) and a failure (0) with probability \(1-p\).

The cumulative number of successes after \(n\) trials is:

\[ B_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i, \]

and the sample mean is:

\[ \bar{Y}_n = \frac{B_n}{n}. \]

The Law of Large Numbers states that:

\[ \bar{Y}_n \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} p \]

i.e. the empirical frequency of successes converges to the true probability \(p\) as the number of trials grows.

3. Server Random Walk (Homework 7) as a Bernoulli Process

In Homework 7, we modelled the weekly status of a server under attack. For each week \(i\) we defined a random variable:

\[ X_i = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{if the server remains secure in week } i, \\ -1, & \text{if the server is breached in week } i. \end{cases} \]

Assuming independence between weeks, we have:

\[ \mathbb{P}(X_i = -1) = q, \quad \mathbb{P}(X_i = +1) = 1 - q, \]

where the effective weekly breach probability is:

\[ q = 1 - (1 - p)^m, \]

with \(p\) the breach probability of a single attacker and \(m\) the number of attackers.

The cumulative score after \(n\) weeks is the random walk:

\[ S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i. \]

Let \(B_n\) be the number of breached weeks (i.e. the number of times \(X_i = -1\)) over \(n\) weeks. Then:

\[ B_n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, q), \qquad S_n = n - 2B_n, \]

so the random walk is just a transformed Binomial variable. The LLN appears again in the limit:

\[ \frac{S_n}{n} = \frac{n - 2B_n}{n} = 1 - 2\frac{B_n}{n} \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} 1 - 2q \]

because \( \tfrac{B_n}{n} \to q \) by the LLN for Bernoulli trials.

4. Analogies Between the Two Simulations

Both the Bernoulli LLN experiment and the server random walk are built on the same core ingredients:

  • a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials,
  • a Binomial distribution obtained by summing these trials,
  • an empirical quantity (mean or normalized sum) that converges to a deterministic limit.

In both cases:

  • each trial/week is a binary event (success/failure or secure/breached),
  • the sum of trials follows a Binomial distribution,
  • the LLN describes the convergence of empirical frequencies or normalized scores to their expectations.

5. Differences Between the Two Simulations

Despite the common structure, the simulations have different interpretations and random variables. The main differences are summarised below.

6. Binomial Coefficients and Pascal’s Triangle

6.1 Binomial Coefficients

In both simulations, the number of successes (or breaches) in \(n\) trials is a Binomial random variable. For the server random walk, let \(B_n\) be the number of breached weeks:

\[ B_n \sim \operatorname{Binomial}(n, q), \]

with probability mass function:

\[ \mathbb{P}(B_n = k) = \binom{n}{k} q^k (1-q)^{n-k}, \quad k = 0, 1, \dots, n. \]

The binomial coefficient \[ \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \] counts how many sequences of length \(n\) contain exactly \(k\) breaches and \(n-k\) secure weeks. This is the combinatorial backbone behind:

  • the histogram of the number of successes \(B_n\) in the LLN homework,
  • the histogram of final scores \(S_n = n - 2k\) in the random walk simulation.

6.2 Pascal’s Triangle (Tartaglia Triangle)

Pascal’s Triangle is a triangular array whose entries are precisely the binomial coefficients \(\binom{n}{k}\). Each entry is the sum of the two entries above it:

\[ \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}, \quad \binom{n}{0} = \binom{n}{n} = 1. \]

This recursive structure has a natural interpretation in terms of paths:

  • start at the top node (0 trials),
  • each step corresponds to adding a trial (or a week),
  • moving “down-left” can represent a breach (or a failure), while “down-right” represents a secure week (or a success),
  • after \(n\) steps, the number of ways to have exactly \(k\) breaches is \(\binom{n}{k}\).

Therefore, every cell in Pascal’s Triangle corresponds to the number of trajectories ending with a particular configuration (number of successes or breaches) in our simulations.

7. Binomial Expansion and Probability

Pascal’s Triangle also encodes the coefficients of the Binomial Theorem:

\[ (a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k. \]

In probabilistic terms, we can choose:

\[ a = 1 - q \quad \text{(probability of a secure week)}, \qquad b = q \quad \text{(probability of a breached week)}. \]

Then:

\[ ( (1-q) + q )^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} (1-q)^{n-k} q^k = 1. \]

Each term

\[ \binom{n}{k} (1-q)^{n-k} q^k \]

represents the probability that exactly \(k\) breaches occur in \(n\) weeks. In the LLN simulation we interpret this as the probability of having \(k\) successes; in the random walk we map the same term to the final score:

\[ S_n = n - 2k. \]

Numerically, when we build histograms from simulations, we are approximating the coefficients of this binomial expansion via empirical frequencies.

8. Fibonacci Sequence and Constrained Paths

The combinatorial structure behind our simple \(\{0,1\}\) or \(\{-1,+1\}\) processes becomes even richer when we introduce constraints on the allowed paths.

  • If we restrict the random walk so that it cannot go below a certain level (for example, we forbid more than one breach in a row, or we do not allow the cumulative score to drop below zero), the number of admissible paths up to step \(n\) often follows classical integer sequences.

Two important examples are:

  • Fibonacci-like counts: in some models where consecutive “down steps” are forbidden, the number of valid sequences of length \(n\) satisfies a recurrence of the form \[ F_{n} = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}, \] producing the Fibonacci sequence.
  • Catalan numbers: if we consider paths that never cross below the starting level (so-called Dyck paths), the number of valid paths with \(2n\) steps is given by the \(n\)-th Catalan number: \[ C_n = \frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}. \]

These connections show that even extremely simple local rules (binary steps) can generate rich families of combinatorial objects, linking probability, number theory and discrete geometry.

9. Overall Conclusions

The Bernoulli LLN simulation and the server random walk from Homework 7 are two faces of the same underlying structure: a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials. Both:

  • are governed by Binomial distributions,
  • have behaviour explained by the Law of Large Numbers,
  • are deeply linked to binomial coefficients, Pascal’s Triangle and the binomial expansion.

Moreover, when we place additional constraints on the trajectories, their counting connects to famous sequences such as Fibonacci and Catalan numbers. This homework therefore emphasizes that:

  • simulations used in probability (LLN, random walks) are not isolated tricks,
  • they sit inside a larger combinatorial framework that unifies binomial coefficients, polynomial identities and classical integer sequences.

In summary, a simple Bernoulli process provides a common language between random experiments, security models and combinatorial mathematics.
